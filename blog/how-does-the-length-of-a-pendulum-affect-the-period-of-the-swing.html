How does the length of a pendulum affect the period of the swing?

By Mark Ng
Published on February 11th, 2020

Research Question: How does the length of a pendulum affect the period of the swing?

Background

Galileo discovered that the time it took for any mass suspended by a string would take the same amount of time to swing from one side to another. He discovered this when he was distracted by a swinging chandelier in church. Using his heartrate as a way of measuring time, he discovered that the chandelier took the same amount of time to swing from one side to the other.

Variables

Independent Variables

  • The length of the string (metres)

    • The string what connects the mass at the bottom to the fixed object, such as a boss and clamp.

      Dependent Variables

  • The period of the pendulum (seconds)

    • This is how long it takes for the pendulum to escape its initial position then return to the maximum height closest to that position in seconds.

      Control variables

  • The mass of the pendulum independent of the string (kilograms).

    • Should pendulums of different masses be used, the gravitational potential energy (and therefore the total kinetic energy) will differ between readings.

  • The method used to measure the period.

    • This is to ensure consistency between different readings, so if the readings are not accurate, they are at least as precise as possible.

  • The fixed object the pendulum is attached to.

    • This is to minimise the possibility of varying frictional behaviour interfering with the results.

Analysis and Conclusion

The graph appears to confirm my hypothesis that the square of the time taken for a suspended mass to oscillate and the suspension length are directly proportional. Between my maximum and minimum graph, there is a possible line of best fit that goes through the origin.

Evaluation

Sources of Error

The length of the string was not accurately measured.

  • I had measured the length of the string using a metre ruler which had to be held mid-air. The pendulum clamp was also in the way and caused the ruler to become slightly askew. This means that my length measurements are unlikely to be within the smallest division on the ruler.

Improvement: Use a more appropriate ruler size such that it is easier to hold or attach a ruler to a boss and clamp so that its placement is both accurate and consistent throughout the experiment.

The angle was not accurately measured.

  • I had to hold a protractor against the pendulum clamp to measure the angle. While this is more accurate than having to hold it mid-air, there are other factors that could have made my measurements inaccurate, such as parallax causing me to be one or two degrees off. The protractor may also not have been held perfectly perpendicular to the clamp on both axes.

Improvement: Attach the protractor to a boss and clamp or to the pendulum clamp if possible and secure it.

The oscillations may not be accurately timed.

  • I used a timer to time the oscillations. While I did try to mitigate the error that this could have caused by recording 5 oscillations instead of 1, there will always be human error. If I used equipment such as an ultrasound sensor, my results would have been far more accurate.

Improvement: Next time, ask for equipment that is more accurate such as an ultrasound sensor. This removes one source of human error.

Sources of Inefficiency

By testing 5 oscillations, more energy is lost to friction at the pivot and drag.

  • Testing 5 oscillations means that the pivot lost 5 times as much energy to air resistance and friction than it would if I did one oscillation. This means that when I divide the total time by 5, it isnâ€™t absolutely equal to the time it would take the mass to oscillate once.

Improvement: Consider performing the experiment in a vacuum so there is no air resistance whatsoever. Though the friction from the pendulum clamp is there, it is so small that is likely negligible.
